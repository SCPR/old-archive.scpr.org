Holiday Reads: Which of the proposed Six Californias should you live in?

If Silicon Valley venture capitalist Tim Draper had his way, California would be split up into six separate states. He plans to get enough signatures to get the “Six Californias” initiative on the November ballot.

And while it’s not yet clear whether that will happen, you can always pretend. Where would you live? Would you move? What are the perks of living in flashy West California versus, say, techie Silicon Valley? Take KPCC’s quiz and find out which hypothetical hexa-state fits you best.

9 ways Southern California could reduce its water use

There’s no shortage of ideas for tackling California’s increasing water shortage, at least based on the wide-ranging comments from five water experts at a recent Crawford Family Forum.

While not exactly demanding that everybody rip out their lawns, the panelists said Southern Californians need to rethink what plants they have in their yards and how they use water overall, such as in appliances and irrigation. They also said not to sweat the relatively “small” utility leaks like the recent one at UCLA. Read about other possible solutions.

'Cantinflas' is the role of a lifetime for Óscar Jaenada

The Mexican actor Mario Moreno was at one time one of the most popular movie stars in the world. His character Cantinflas was a pauper who faced authorities and fought injustice through slapstick, improvisation and wordplay, and came to represent Mexico itself.

In the new biopic on the actor, Óscar Jaenada portays Moreno and Cantinflas, who he says were two distinct characters. He tells KPCC of the responsibility he felt to do justice to the Mexican icon when Jaenada himself is Spanish, eventually moving to Mexico to immerse himself in the role. Listen to his interview.

Free Weekend: Race the river, feel the funk, see a giant duck

You’ve worked hard, and you deserve a break—a government-sanctioned one. This Labor Day weekend, avoid the crowds at the Made in America Festival Downtown and check out some of these cheap outings.

Feel the funk with George Clinton and others at Funk Fest at the Queen Mary.

The pair who ignite the Hollywood Bowl's fireworks

Some come for the music, some for the picnic. But for many Angelenos, summer at the Hollywood Bowl is all about the fireworks.

And contrary to what some concertgoers believe, the pyrotechnics are precisely choreographed, not by a computer, but by Eric Elias and musical score reader Sara Hiner, a classically trained bassoonist who reads the score and watches the conductor in order to call out the cues for Elias to push the red button that send the fireworks flying. For immigrant parents, a question of schooling

A recent report from the Migration Policy Institute found children of recently arrived immigrants are being left out of the nation’s expanding early education system.

That presents a challenge for educators: how to convince parents that early education in an institutional setting, where children won't necessarily learn about their culture and language, will be positive for the child. One training program, the "bridging cultures project," aims to help by showing teachers how to engage diverse families and by encouraging them to invite parents into the classroom.

The mystery of Death Valley's 'wandering stones' solved

For half a century, the sliding rocks of Racetrack Playa in Death Valley National Park had remained a mystery. No one had seen how these hundreds of heavy stones made their jagged trek across the dry desert lake bed—until now.

In a study published this week in PLOS ONE, paleobiologist Richard Norris used GPS trackers and cameras to film the rocks moving. According to his findings, thin slabs of ice that sit atop water in certain areas get broken up as breezes push the ice across the playa, eventually “bulldozing” through the rocks. See video of the stones in action, slow as it is.

Hello Kitty is not a cat, goodbye sanity

The Internet—nay, the world—is still reeling from the news this week that Hello Kitty is not actually a cat, which for some people apparently called into question existence itself.

So if Kitty is not a cat, what is she? Anthropologist and Hello Kitty expert Christine Yano, who brought the news to light, tells KPCC she is a friend, an icon, a celebrity “and maybe even a girl.” She is British, her real name is Kitty White and she has a twin named Mimmy. Yano says the character is part of the Japanese culture’s focus on “asobi,” the spirit of play or fantasy where not everything needs to make sense. Listen to her interview.

You’ve been eating sushi the wrong way

This has been a week of revelations. Ice makes those mysterious rocks in Death Valley move. Hello Kitty is not a cat. And you don’t know how to eat sushi.

Munchies, Vice’s food website, sought some wasabi wisdom from Tokyo sushi chef Naomichi Yasuda on the proper way to eat sushi. The first rule: eat cut rolls with your hands, not chopsticks. For nigiri, turn it on its side, then upside down and dip the fish—not the rice—in the soy sauce. Never put sliced ginger on the sushi itself. And don’t shake that soy sauce off, for sake’s sake. That’s just gauche gourmandizing. See how your sushi-eating experience has been a sham.

Visions of virtual reality through the decades

Virtual reality is everywhere. From Oculus Rift to VR headsets you can make with a smartphone, the technology has gone from sci-fi fantasy to the real world.

It’s long been an obsession for writers and scientists who’ve imagined creating whole worlds out of nothing. The Verge has a visual timeline of some of those moments during the last century, where people have imagined the virtual world, often nefariously, taking over the real one. It begins with Ray Bradbury’s inchoate version of VR, all the way through "The Matrix," and to a helpful guide on how to make your own headset.

Celebrating Marvel Comics legend Jack Kirby

Thursday would have been the 97th birthday of the late Jack Kirby, considered by many the greatest comic book artist of all time. He co-created classic Marvel Comics characters including Captain America, the Fantastic Four, the X-Men, Iron Man, Thor and the Hulk.

Among the lesser-known facts about "King" Kirby: He created "Guardians of the Galaxy" character Groot; did storyboards for the fake sci-fi film used as part of a CIA operation depicted in the movie "Argo"; and co-created influential DC Comics characters, too. Read on for more.

How to pursue a complaint against your health insurer

Health coverage is complicated. A rising number of consumer calls and complaints to the state's two industry watchdogs, the Department of Managed Health Care and the California Department of Insurance, bears that out.

So what do you do if you have a problem with your insurer? Here are some tips: Know your plan — learn the coverage details. Keep track of your communications — record representative names and call dates. File a complaint with DMHC or CDI. Or get an Independent Medical Review. Read on for more.

Kiddie cocktails mix fun and fizz

Hold the booze, please. Stuart Sandler, the author of "Kiddie Cocktails," a fabulous, illustrated collection of alcohol-free drinks, recently demonstrated his virgin mixology at KPCC.

Among Sandler's benign concoctions are "The Cotton Candy" and "The Orange Dream." With the first, you stuff a hurricane glass full of cotton candy and slowly pour Sprite or 7-Up over the top, watching the sugary fibers dissolve. The second combines orange and cream sodas for a drinkable creamsicle. Read on for more recipes and to see Sandler at work.

Science crowns mozzarella the king of pizza cheese

The signature of a great American-style pizza is not the toppings du jour but the cheese: hot, gooey mozzarella, with big, dark splotches of caramelization.

Pizzerias didn't happen upon that winning recipe by coincidence. Food scientists have been studying and finessing the low-moisture part-skim mozzarella we now put on most of our pizzas for decades.

The unique browning patterns on mozzarella come from the way it bubbles. Since it's made by repeatedly stretching and molding fresh curds, "mozzarella has a lot of elasticity."
