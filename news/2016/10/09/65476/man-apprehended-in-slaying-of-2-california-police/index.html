Man apprehended in slaying of 2 Palm Springs police officers


Robert Jablon and John Rogers with KPCC Staff | AP | October 9, 2016

In the minutes before three Palm Springs officers were shot, two fatally, the suspected gunman's father told a neighbor his son was armed, "acting crazy" and wanted to shoot police.

John Felix, 26, was arrested early Sunday after a lengthy standoff and faces charges including two counts of murder on a peace officer. Felix was wearing body armor and carrying several high capacity magazines when he was apprehended by police, according to Riverside County Sheriff's Department Chief Deputy Ray Wood.

Police said Felix suddenly pulled out a gun and opened fire on the officers who had responded to a family disturbance call Saturday afternoon at the home in a quiet neighborhood of this desert resort city.

A neighbor, Frances Serrano, told The Associated Press that the suspect's panicked father, Santos Felix, said his son, an admitted gang member, had a gun.

"My son is inside and we're scared, he's acting crazy," Serrano said the older Felix told her. When it was suggested they call the police he said, "Yeah, he already knows they are coming, and he is going to shoot them."

Serrano said she went back inside her house and within minutes police cars arrived and gunfire erupted.

Palm Springs police Chief Bryan Reyes identified the slain officers as Jose "Gil" Gilbert Vega and Lesley Zerebny.

Zerebny, 27, had been with the department for about 18 months and only recently returned from maternity leave after giving birth to a daughter four months ago. Vega, a married father of eight, was a 35-year veteran who planned to retire in December. He had been working overtime on his day off Saturday.

The wounded officer's name was not released. He is "alert" and aiding investigators with information and could be released from the hospital Sunday afternoon, Reyes said in a news conference.

Police arrested Felix, who was taken to a hospital for treatment of injuries that did not appear to be life-threatening. After trying for several hours to make contact with Felix, they fired "chemical agents" into the residence where he was barricading himself, Wood said. Felix then exited through the back door and was shot with non-lethal munitions before being subdued and arrested, Wood said.

Reyes indicated police had had previous dealings with the suspect, but declined to elaborate. Riverside County District Attorney Michael Hestrin said in a news conference that he will file charges against Felix on Tuesday. Felix will be arraigned on Wednesday or Thursday.

Hestrin said he expects to charge Felix with two counts of first-degree murder. The charges will include special circumstances relating to multiple murder and the murder of police officers, making Felix eligible for the death penalty. A decision on whether to pursue the death penalty will be made in the next few weeks.

"I consider a brutal murder of a police officer to be a very heinous crime, so Ill leave it at that, Hestrin said.

Court records show Felix is a gang member who was previously sentenced to four years in prison in a failed murder plot in 2009. Documents cited by the Desert Sun newspaper reveal Felix was charged with attempted murder but pleaded down to assault with a firearm and admitted his gang connection.

Documents also show Felix was the subject of a forceful arrest three years ago at the same house where Saturday's shootings occurred.

Riverside County SWAT officers quickly sealed off the residential neighborhood as police evacuated some residents. They told others to stay inside their homes, keep their doors locked and not to open them for anyone.

As the lockdown continued, scores of police officers gathered at Palm Springs Desert Regional Medical Center to offer a somber salute as the bodies of Zerebny and Vega were loaded into white hearses for transport to a coroner's office.

It had been 54 years since an on-duty uniformed police officer was killed in Palm Springs, a city of 45,000 residents about 100 miles east of Los Angeles, known for its desert views, boutique hotels and golf courses.

In front of police headquarters, scores of local residents gathered to leave flowers, balloons and cards.

Vega had submitted his paperwork to retire at the end of the year after a long and decorated career, Reyes said.

"Here he is, 35 years in, still pushing a patrol car for our community to make it better on a day he wasn't even scheduled to work," the chief said.

Reyes called Zerebny a "wonderful, young, dedicated female officer that pressed forward every day to make it better for everybody else." She and her husband, a Riverside County sheriff's deputy, were new parents to a four-month-old baby.

To see her laying down with her eyes open and to witness her husband, in full Riverside County sheriffs uniform, kiss her on the forehead for the last time, said Palm Springs police chief Bryan Reyes, andthen Reyes had to pause, his eyes tearing up.Its tough, he finallysaid.

"I don't even remember anything so vicious and cruel," said Palm Springs resident Heidi Thompson. "These officers are responding to a domestic call for somebody in need that they don't even know. They put their life on the line for us, the community. And they get gunned down? I don't understand it."

The shooting occurred just three days after a popular Los Angeles County sheriff's sergeant was shot and killed in the high desert town of Lancaster.

Sgt. Steve Owen was answering a burglary call when sheriff's officials say he was shot by a man who then stood over him and shot him four more times.

A paroled robber has been charged with murder.

Hundreds of mourners and well-wishers have been making a pilgrimage Sunday to the courtyard in front of police headquarters in Palm Springs, laying flowers at a makeshift memorial for the two police officers killed yesterday, and a third officer who was shot and injured.A candlelight vigil will be held in front of the Palm Springs Police Department at 5 p.m. Sunday.

Among the mourners wasMaria Canas, who lives just down the street from the station.

This is terrible. We pass by here every day and we say hi, theyre very nice, Canas said. We feel very bad, and we are praying for the families because they are suffering a lot right now.

That was really rough, said Sandra Walmsley of Indian Wells, after dropping her flowers at the memorial. The idea of this four-month-old waiting at home for a mom who didnt get to come home, its just very tragic, and incredibly sad.

Walmsley has lived in the Palm Springs area for nine years and calls it a close-knit small community -- and it has never seen anything like this, she said.

You think a place like this is immune to these things, she said. But were not immune. This is happening everywhere.

The neighbors who live near the crime scene -- near Cypress Road, about three miles north of police headquarters -- didnt want to discuss the event on the record, but the overall description of what happened yesterday afternoon is that it was loud and unmistakable. Residents said they were stunned by the intense popping of the gunfire itself and by how many shots were fired.

"I live right here," said Julie Hessen, pointing to the senior centera few doors down from the police station. She said she hasloved the small-town feel ofPalm Springs since her family moved here in 1957."You go to the market, you see people you knew in high school," she said.

Hessen said it's hard not to be able to do anything about it.

"I thought about offering to take care of that four-month-old for awhile, but I'm sure they have family," Hessen said. "I just wish there was something I could do."

Weber and Associated Press writer John Rogers reported from Los Angeles. Associated Press writers Olga R. Rodriguez in San Francisco and Adam Kealoha Causey in Phoenix also contributed to this story.
