LA sues to shut down alleged medical pot delivery app

Elizabeth Aguilera | December 2, 2014

Listen to story

00:57
Download this story 0MB

Los Angeles City Attorney Mike Feuer filed a lawsuit Tuesday seeking to shut down a mobile app company called Nestdrop for offering home delivery of medical marijuana. The suit also seeks financial penalties. Nestdrop denies it is involved in the delivery of medical pot.

Feuer's suit is his latest move in a crackdown on medical marijuana dispensaries that he began when he took office 17 months ago. Since then, his office has closed 402 medical pot shops - about half of L.A.'s dispensaries, he said.

Feuer said the stepped up enforcement is a result of the passage of Proposition D last year. The proposition restricts the number of dispensaries to 2007 levels, regulates where dispensaries can be located and requires registration with the city.

Feuer filed his suit against Nestdrop in Los Angeles Superior Court. The mobile app drew the city attorney’s attention last month when it announced it would begin facilitating the delivery of medical marijuana along with the alcohol it already offers.

"There are many categories of marijuana, both edible and otherwise, that are purported to be available through this mobile app," Feuer said. "Prop D is very clear, there is no lawful delivery service."

Nestdrop said in a statement that it is only a "technology platform" that connects patients with local dispensaries.  According to Nestdrop, it does not fulfill orders or deliver product – that is done by a local dispensary.

"We are saddened by the City Attorney's recent attempt to restrict patient’s access to their legal medicine and intend to fight this," co-founder Michael Pycher said in the statement.

Feuer said his office is also investigating other medical marijuana services that advertise delivery.  Under Proposition D, Feuer said, only patients with medical cards and their caregivers may transport medical marijuana.

Calls to several firms that offer medical marijuana delivery were not returned.

Of the 402 dispensaries his office has closed, Feuer said they "either didn’t comply with registration and business tax requirements of Prop D, [and/or] they may have been too close to another medical marijuana dispensaries or a school or a playground or a religious institution or a child care center."

The city attorney's office said it has filed 200 criminal cases against dispensary operators and property owners as part of the crackdown.
