The fight over the Santa Monica Mountain wine industry


Jed Kim | May 9, 2014

Listen to story

04:21
Download this story 6MB

Tim Skogstrom didn’t think much of Santa Monica wines the first time he tried them.

“I literally thought that they were horrible,” said the owner of the Cornell Winery, a tasting room in the Santa Monica Mountains.

Two years later, he gave them another try, and it changed his life.

“[The] geometric leap in quality blew me away. That’s when I made my decision to go after something like this, because I saw that if they were able to take it from here to here in that short period of time, they’re serious about it,” Skogstrom said. “Where would it be another two years down the line?”

Skogstrom opened his business a few years later. Part of his mission was to feature wines produced from grapes grown in the Santa Monica Mountains.

His business has been growing as more people discover the wine country just a short drive away from metropolitan Los Angeles. The future of the local wine industry, however, is in question, as a result of a coastal development and land use plan that is close to being implemented.

A long time coming

The plan in question is the "local coastal program," or LCP, for the Santa Monica Mountains. The LCP is essentially a guideline for development in the patch of the coastal zone that runs through the Santa Monica Mountains.

The California Coastal Act mandates that cities and counties develop an LCP that properly balances competing interests of environmental protection, commercial development, agriculture and recreational access.

County officials said that getting stakeholders to agree on a plan that the Coastal Commission would approve has been very difficult.

“When I first took office in 1994, this was one of the things that I said I wanted to accomplish, and this is the last piece of business that I will be able to accomplish, and it’s maybe the most important one,” said County Supervisor Zev Yaroslavsky, who represents the area in question.

Currently, applicants hoping to develop projects on land within the coastal zone must seek approval from both the county and the California Coastal Commission. Once a local coastal program is passed, applicants will only need to obtain county permits.

“The average homeowner who wants to add onto their home or build a new home where that would be permitted will have the opportunity to do that by going through one level of government, not two. It’ll be a considerable savings to them in terms of money and time,” Yaroslavsky said.

The program is nearer to completion than previous attempts. In April, the California Coastal Commission unanimously approved the land use plan, which is a major part of the LCP. The remaining portion of the program will go before the Coastal Commission in July. If it passes, it will go to the Board of Supervisors for final approval and could be in place by summer.  

Environmental concern

The proposed LCP is widely supported by environmentalists as well as some homeowner and equestrian groups. Many wine growers, however, oppose the land use plan, because it bans any new vineyards from being planted and allows the county to go after unpermitted vineyards.

Coastal chaparral, which is considered sensitive and rare, must be cleared in order to plant a vineyard. Researchers said vineyards can also cause erosion and negative impacts to streams.

“They’ve seen pool habitats and other important habitats for species like newts in our Santa Monica Mountains completely fill up and biodiversity decline severely in those areas,” said Sarah Sikich, science and policy director for coastal resources at Heal the Bay, which supports the proposed local coastal program.

Yaroslavsky said that he’s concerned vineyards would also change the visual aesthetics of the mountains.

“This is probably the most expansive and significant national park and national recreation area adjacent to a metropolitan area anywhere in the United States, and it deserves to be protected,“ Yaroslavsky said. “If we don’t, future generations will never forgive us,”

A messy fight

Some vintners said they are being unfairly singled out by the proposed LCP. They argue that concerns over vineyards’ environmental impacts are either overblown or poorly understood.

“We don’t share those beliefs. We don’t think that they’re scientifically justified, and the agrarian lifestyle here in the Santa Monica Mountains predates everything. It goes back to recorded history out here, and we’re not just going to go quietly into the night,” said Don Schmitz, a representative of the Coastal Coalition of Family Farmers.

Schmitz owns a nearly 40-acre patch of land in the Santa Monica Mountains. He grows grapes on a few acres surrounding his house and sells wine under the label of Malibu Solstice. Since he has the proper permits, his vineyards will be grandfathered in if the local coastal program passes.

Despite that, Schmitz opposes the plan, saying that it doesn’t adhere to the California Coastal Act. He said it’s ridiculous to think that vineyards would ever take over the Santa Monica Mountains and that it’s possible to grow grapes responsibly. He said the LCP should go for limitations, not a ban.

“It’s a big country. These are big mountains. The chaparral that they’re talking about is the most common biome, or one of the most common biomes, in the state of California, and the government owns the vast majority of it. Isn’t there enough room in a land use plan for everybody to coexist? I think that there is,” Schmitz said.

The fight over grape growing in the Santa Monica Mountains has been a messy one, with recriminations coming from both sides. County Supervisor Yaroslavsky said Schmitz’s motivations for opposing the plan have little to do with the rights of vintners. He noted that Schmitz is a consultant who works with developers looking to build in the coastal zone.

“The one person who’s largely been attacking the prohibition of vineyards also professionally represents people who want to do major developments in the mountains, and I believe in my heart of hearts that the complaint about the vineyard prohibition has really been a stalking horse to try to kill the plan,” Yaroslavsky said.

Schmitz dismissed Yaroslavsky’s claim, saying he also does plenty of work helping people comply with LCPs. He said that he only has the rights of agriculture in mind.

“I think it's utter nonsense. I have plenty of work all up and down the state. And besides, the local coastal program is a very complex document. We provide land use planning services to property owners,” Schmitz said. “I don’t have the slightest worry that when the local coastal program is adopted that we will lack for business.”

The future of Santa Monica Mountain wine industry

It’s difficult to know how many vineyards actually exist in the Santa Monica Mountains. Several of them are hobby vineyards that lack permits. Tim Skogstrom estimated that about 50 exist and that the entire region produces about 15,000 cases of wine each year.

Though that amount pales in comparison to the output of larger wine regions, Skogstrom said that the quality of many Santa Monica Mountain wines is world class.

He said, though, that the industry will likely suffer if new vineyards are banned.

“It just means that we’re never going to grow the business. The business is just going to become this static entity, and static entities tend to just die off,” Skogstrom said.
