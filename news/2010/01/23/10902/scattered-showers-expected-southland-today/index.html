Another round of storms moves through Southern California

| January 22, 2010

Listen to story

01:24
Download this story 0MB

Updated at 6:07 p.m. | Permalink

Utility crews working on SoCal storm outages

More than 15,000 Southern California Edison customers remain without service after this week's storms and the utility says crews will work through the weekened to restore power.

Lightning, hail, snow and strong winds contributed to the outages.

SoCal Edison says as of Friday afternoon 3,150 customers remain without electricity in Yucaipa, which awakened to a rare blanket of snow.

The other large outage is in the Blythe area where Interstate 10 reaches the Arizona state line in southeastern California.

SoCal Edison says more than 90 power poles were blown down in the Blythe area on Thursday.
-Associated Press

Updated at 5:47 p.m. | Permalink
Red Cross evacuation centers closed
All eight Red Cross shelters serving the evacuated areas have been closed. Officials warned of the threat of thunderstorms in the weeks to come and said the Red Cross will remain on stand-by.

La Cañada High School, 4463 Oak Grove Dr. La Cañada Flintridge — CLOSED

Sunland Recreation Center, 8651 Foothill Blvd. Sunland — CLOSED

Anderson Senior Center, 828 S. Mesa Street. San Pedro — CLOSED

Marie Kerr Park, 39700 30th Street West. Palmdale - CLOSED

Bridgeport Memorial Auditorium, 73 N. School Street. Bridgeport - CLOSED

Glendale Civic Auditorium, 1401 North Verdugo Road. Glendale - CLOSED

Sierra Madre Recreation Center, 611 East Sierra Madre Boulevard. Sierra Madre - CLOSED

Lee Vining Community Center, 296 Mattly Avenue. Lee Vining - CLOSED

-KPCC Wire Services

Updated at 5:20 p.m. | Permalink
CSULB cleans up from storm damage.
Dozens of workers and contractors are helping Cal State Long Beach clean up the damage from this week’s storms. A student union lounge thousands of people use each day will be out of commission for a month. During a fresh burst of rain, university spokeswoman Toni Beron said the downpour on Tuesday overwhelmed a storm drain below the bottom floor.

"We took on about 8-10 inches of water on the second and third floors from downhill runoff. On the first floor of the union we had a flood of about 18 inches of water that took over nearly the entire floor. What happened, a pipe broke under the floor but we didn’t know where and we had water gushing like a geyser through the floor and lifting the floor up," Beron said.

Beron estimated that the repair bill will run into the hundreds of thousands of dollars. Most students were on break this week. A key intersection near the university’s Atherton Street entrance also flooded. Continuing rains worry administrators because on Monday more than 30,000 students return to classes for the spring semester.
-Adolfo Guzman Lopez

Updated at 4:03 p.m. | Permalink
Residents relieved no major mudslides

Families in the foothills are relieved that no major mudslides have followed this week’s storms. Officials are letting most evacuated residents back into their homes today.

Some streets remain off limits though. Local authorities issued evacuation orders for more than 1,900 homes this week. Sheriff’s deputies are checking identification at entry points back into evacuated areas, to ensure that only the occupants may return.

L.A. County Sheriff’s Division Chief Neal Tyler says he thinks the region dodged a bullet this time.
“Partly because the Department of Public Works flood control system works so well, partly because this series of storms didn’t present the threat as predicted. And partly blind luck. You can’t predict every aspect of slope failure even today in 2010.”

But L.A. County Sheriff’s Division Chief Neal Tyler says it would be a mistake for residents to think authorities were crying wolf in ordering evacuations …

"We don’t evacuate people for fun. We do it for public safety," he says. "And my message to people who feel merely because we didn’t see a massive disaster this time that they don’t need to leave next time is that’s very short sighted and dangerous."

There is more good news following the heavy rainfall. The drought stricken region has more water now. L.A. public works officials said they captured enough water during these storms to serve 150,000 families for a year.

Specific information on evacuation areas is available onlin at dpwcare.org.

- Shirley Jahad

Updated at 2:05 p.m. | Permalink
Most evacuation orders lifted, but officials weren't kidding about their seriousness

Hundreds of people in the foothills north of Los Angeles are getting back into their homes today. L.A. County officials have lifted evacuation orders in many areas that were prone to mudslide damage. Homes in the most precarious areas are still off limits.

No major mudslides followed the storms this week. Officials say they’re relieved. But they don’t want foothill dwellers to think they were crying wolf.

"We don’t evacuate people for fun," said Neal Tyler, an L.A. County Sheriff division chief. "We do it for public safety. And my message to people who feel merely because we didn’t see a massive disaster this time that they don’t need to leave next time is that’s very short-sighted and dangerous."

Check the Web site DPWCare.org for the official list of addresses that are still under evacuation orders.

- KPCC staff

Updated at 1:48 p.m. | Permalink
Dog rescued in Vernon by fire helicopter crew

A dog walking in the Los Angeles River today near Vernon was rescued by a city fire helicopter crew.

The dog was reported walking in several inches of water about 11:30 a.m. near Washington Blvd., said Erik Scott of the Los Angeles Fire Department.

The "river" is actually a flood control channel that is usually dry, except when it rains, as it has for the past five days.

The sides of the concrete ditch are steep and the dog couldn't climb out.

A bridge was shut down as fire engines, ambulances and helicopters – including media choppers – swarmed the scene.

The dog ran away from initial rescue attempts, but eventually a firefighter dropped into the water from a helicopter and tackled the dog, which put up a bit of a fight before being lifted to safety.

The dog was last seen being placed into an ambulance to be checked out.

The firefighter was also examined – for a possible dog bite.

- KPCC Wire Services

Updated at 1:39 p.m. | Permalink
Potential disaster has politicians heading for hills

Governor Arnold Schwarzenegger flew back from Washington Thursday night in case the week’s rainstorms trigger mudslides that warrant a statewide disaster declaration.

Republican Congressman David Dreier, whose San Gabriel Valley district includes areas under threat of mudslides, has also returned to California. Officials have lifted most evacuation orders. But if the mud does start to move, Dreier urges his constituents to get out of the way.

"You know there have been evacuations of over a thousand homes and a lot of people have been concerned about it," said Dreier. "And an important message I try to get out is as unpleasant as it is, the threat of massive mudslides with the potential damage of property and loss of life is something that we need to be mindful of."

Dreier spoke on the flight from Washington. He’s on his way to the foothills to see for himself what steps emergency officials have taken to mitigate potential mudslide damage.

- KPCC staff

Updated at 1:24 p.m. | Permalink
SAG Awards add tent to protect celebs from rain

Hoping to avoid the proliferation of umbrellas that soured the Golden Globe Awards red carpet, the Screen Actors Guild announced today that it planned to put a tent over the red carpet to keep celebrities dry as they walk into the SAG Awards ceremony at the Shrine Auditorium.

The rain is expected to dissipate by the time the awards ceremony is held at 5 p.m. Saturday, but SAG officials said they were taking no chances and would tent the red carpet.

Sunday's rain cast a pall over the Golden Globe red carpet, with stars being handed umbrellas as they stepped out of limousines. Some of the attendees – particularly actresses wearing pricey gowns and sporting glamorous hairstyles – questioned why a tent wasn't erected over the red carpet.

- KPCC Wire Services

Updated at 10:56 a.m. | Permalink

Some evacuation orders will be lifted today, including in Acton and La Canada-Flintridge, with the addresses of homes that can safely be re-occupied to be listed this morning on a Los Angeles County Web site, dpwcare.org, according to county officials.

In the mountains, more heavy snow and gusty winds were expected through this evening, creating perilous traveling conditions, according to the National Weather Service.

About 11,000 utility customers from Los Angeles to the Colorado River were without electricity this morning due to storm-related outages. The total included about 1,300 Department of Water and Power customers in the city of Los Angeles and around another 9,600 outside L.A. in areas served by Southern California Edison.

NWS forecasters said showers would be heavy at times today, and scattered thunderstorms were expected in mountain areas.

The NWS issued a flash flood watch for the San Fernando, Santa Clarita, San Gabriel and Pomona valleys and the mountains of Los Angeles County effective through this evening, and a high surf advisory for the Los Angeles County coast until 4 a.m. Sunday.

In Orange County coastal areas, a flash flood watch will be in effect through this afternoon, a coastal flood warning until 4 a.m. Saturday, and a high surf advisory until 2 a.m. Monday.

The snow level will be around 3,000 feet today, dropping to 2,500 feet in some areas. NWS forecasters said snowfall would have an impact on traffic along Interstate 5 in the area of The Grapevine and possibly along portions of the 14 freeway.

"The combination of snow and blowing snow will reduce visibility to near zero at times," according to an NWS advisory. "Traveling through the mountains is highly discouraged through Friday night as near-blizzard conditions can be expected at times."

In the mountains of Ventura and Los Angeles counties, with the exception of the Santa Monica range, snow accumulation of 6 to 12 inches was expected, along with winds of between 20 and 35 miles per hour, gusting to around 55 mph, according to the NWS.

Despite the departure of the latest storm's main front, a high level of concern remained regarding the fate of properties in foothill communities below the site of last year's 250-square-mile Station Fire.

Los Angeles County Supervisor Mike Antonovich said Thursday nearly 2,000 homes were under evacuation orders because of fears of mud and debris flows. Officials said slides could occur 72 hours after rain stops falling, and there was no immediate indication when the evacuation orders would be lifted.

Another storm could hit the region late Monday night or Tuesday, but it could miss the Greater Los Angeles Area and was expected to be weaker than the systems that battered the Southland this week, the NWS reported.

- KPCC Wire Services
